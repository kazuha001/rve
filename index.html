Introduction

The term "Reformed" originated in the 16th century to describe churches that reorganized based on theological principles distinct from the Roman Catholic Church, originally known as "churches that are Reformed on the basis of Scripture." Over time, the shorter form "Reformed" became widely used by many churches globally. These churches emphasize doctrines rediscovered during the Reformation and declare that the Bible is the foundation of their faith and structure. However, as time passes, the connection between the original meaning of "Reformed" and its current use risks becoming weaker.

Biblical Reform

The Latin word “reformare” appears only twice in the Bible, but the concept of reform is present throughout both the Old and New Testaments. True reformation, as shown in Scripture, occurs when God guides His people back to His Word and proper worship of Him. Examples include the reforms of Asa, Josiah, and those recorded in Nehemiah, where God’s laws and worship were restored. The 16th-century Reformation was a similar movement, where churches returned to biblical principles and worship. Theologically, "reformation" means restoring proper service to God, rooted in Scripture.

Reformation in the Sixteenth Century

It's unclear if the term "reformation" took on a more specific meaning by the 16th century, as it likely retained its broader medieval sense of "renewal." The reformers did not create the term to describe their efforts. Erasmus, for example, focused on personal conversion and repentance, while Martin Luther rarely used the word. Luther initially applied "reformation" to changes in university regulations to encourage modern study methods. When he posted his 95 theses in 1517, he aimed for academic debate, not a split from the Roman Church. He had no idea this event would later mark the start of the Reformation.

Radical Reformation

The word "reform" became popular because the reformers emphasized that their changes were structural adjustments to the existing church based on biblical doctrine, not a complete rejection of it. However, the Roman Church portrayed them as radicals breaking away from the universal ("catholic") church. To counter this, reformers frequently cited early church fathers like Jerome and Augustine to show continuity with the historic church. Even today, Reformed churches identify with the church of all ages, rooted in the same biblical and theological tradition.

Reformed Churches

In the 1520s, the term "reformed" wasn't used in Germany for congregations implementing change; instead, they were called "protestant." At the 1529 Diet of Speyer, south German cities protested the reinstatement of Roman authority, using "protest" in its original positive sense, meaning "to profess or witness" their belief that reforms were God's will. The term "protestant" later fell out of use as divisions arose, and it wasn't until the 19th century that "Protestantism" became a common term for churches that had separated from the Roman Catholic Church.

Reformation as Historical Term

In English, "Reformation" refers to the Protestant changes in the 16th century, specifically from 1517 to 1600. The term became widely used after historian Leopold von Ranke's 1839 work. It denotes the broad religious and church changes across Europe during this period, not just the reforms of individuals like Zwingli, Knox, or Calvin. While initially focused on religious and theological changes, the term "Reformation" now also includes cultural, social, economic, and secular developments of the time, as historians explore the wider impact of the era.

Counter-Reformation

Many modern historians find the term "Counter-Reformation" misleading because it often describes changes in the Roman Catholic Church that were not necessarily anti-Reformed in motivation. They argue that these changes were part of a positive development that began before the 16th century, rather than being purely reactionary. Additionally, the term carries negative connotations, suggesting opposition to reform, which makes it less appealing compared to the innovations of Protestant reformers. This perception has led to an imbalance in scholarship, focusing more on Protestantism and neglecting Roman Catholic reforms, which are essential for a comprehensive understanding of the Reformation era.

Catholic Reformation

Historians have shifted from using "Counter-Reformation" to "Catholic Reformation" to describe the changes in the Roman Catholic Church aimed at maintaining its viability during the 16th century. This term is particularly relevant for Italy and Spain, where significant reforms occurred, explaining why the Protestant Reformation had little impact in those regions and why Roman Catholicism remained largely unchallenged. Understanding the Catholic Reformation helps clarify the varying extent and nature of the Reformation across Europe, such as in Poland, where a resurgence of Catholicism prevented the institutionalization of Protestant reforms. Thus, "Catholic Reformation" is an appropriate term for the period of change within the Roman Church from 1550 to 1650.

Second Reformation

The period from 1550 to 1700 is referred to as the "Second Reformation" by some writers, distinguishing reforms from Geneva during this time from those of the first generation of reformers. Others use the term to describe the Pietism movement in northern Germany and the Netherlands, which emphasized religious experience. However, the term has its critics, as this period lacked major theological discoveries that led to significant ecclesiastical reform. Critics argue that the changes made during this time were not substantial enough to justify calling it a "reformation." Thus, its long-term acceptance as a term remains uncertain.

Conclusion

Modern Reformed churches assert that they are continually reformed by the Word of God, recognizing that their identity is rooted in the 16th-century Reformation while remaining open to ongoing changes in doctrine and practice. They acknowledge imperfections within the "catholic" church and emphasize the importance of understanding historical reforms throughout the ages, including those in the church fathers and the Middle Ages.

While the Reformation was a significant event that established essential biblical doctrines, it is crucial for contemporary Reformed churches to appreciate the Roman Catholic teachings that prompted reformers to advocate for change and to pray for a return to biblical truth.

Moreover, the Reformed churches must understand their place within the broader context of Protestantism, recognizing the social, political, and geographical factors that led to diverse reformations in different regions, such as Scotland, Hungary, and the Netherlands. While maintaining a common foundation in returning to Scripture, it is important not to overstate the Reformation's significance. The ongoing guidance of God in His church is crucial, and believers are called to serve Him humbly as "unworthy servants."
